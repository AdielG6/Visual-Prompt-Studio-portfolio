Pitaya: What You Call Yellow Dragon Fruit by Daniela Maria Ferreira C-V, Art Gallery, Dallas College

Porcelain, glaze,

The artwork consists of two glazed porcelain sculptural forms with an organic appearance resembling enlarged fruit or seed pods. One form stands upright and split open, revealing a hollow interior, while the other rests horizontally as a detached segment. Together, they suggest a moment of growth, rupture, or transformation, as if a natural process has been paused in time. The forms appear grown rather than manufactured, reinforcing their biological character.

Irregular lines and textured ridges animate the surfaces. Actual lines are visible along the physical edges, especially where the upright form opens. Implied lines emerge from the flowing curves, guiding the viewer’s eye across the surface and into the interior cavity. The vertical orientation of the larger piece creates directional movement upward and inward, while contour lines emphasize the rounded, uneven volume.

Organic shapes dominate the composition. The porcelain structures function as positive shapes, while negative space appears between them and within the hollow interior. The interaction between solid mass and void creates balance, making the empty space as significant as the physical form. This relationship encourages exploration of both interior and exterior surfaces.

Light and shadow interact naturally with the textured glaze. Raised ridges catch highlights, while recessed grooves and the cavity fall into shadow. Depth is conveyed through scale, placement, and tonal variation. The larger upright form contrasts with the smaller horizontal segment, establishing proportion and spatial tension.

The color palette consists of warm, analogous yellows and browns. Muted tones reinforce the earthy, organic quality of the sculpture. Although physically still, the opened form and detached segment suggest implied motion. Unity emerges through repeated organic shapes, consistent texture, and harmonious color, while asymmetrical balance and enlarged scale transform seed-like forms into monumental symbols of identity and vulnerability.
